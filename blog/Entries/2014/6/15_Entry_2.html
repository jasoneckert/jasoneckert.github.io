Les Misérables (2012) movie review

Sunday, June 15, 2014

 

This post is a departure from my typical tech-oriented material.


Les Misérables is my favorite musical - when I was in high school, it was the focus of our performances for my senior two years, and I’ve loved it ever since.  I was the pianist for our band groups and know every song by heart.


Recently, when my daughter backed out of a free ticket to the premiere of Les Misérables

at the Dunfield Theatre in Cambridge, I took her place.  The performance was perhaps the best I’ve seen.....ever!  And I’ve seen plenty of musicals ;-)


Of course, that led me to Google for some academic pursuits, and I immediately noticed there was a Les Misérables movie released in 2012 starring Hugh Jackman, and decided to watch it.  To say that the movie was disappointing would be an understatement.


The biggest problem with this film is that it suffers from identity crisis.  It doesn’t know if it’s a movie or a musical.....and this confusion lasts throughout.  Both the story and emotional impact is lost as a result.  The movie is performed and sung like a musical, but with the closeups and scenery panning of a movie.  Many parts are over-acted, slow-paced, too serious, and poorly fitted to the story.  The WOW factor that you get in the musical is gone.


When I was a kid, I watched The Pirates of Penzance musical on TV, and it was great!  It wasn’t ultra-realistic and was filmed in a way that made you feel as if you were in a theatre watching a musical.  As a result, you were able to focus on the acting and music just like at a live musical.  I still remember the part where the flowers were singing - a crude stage effect, but hilarious!  


However, the producers of the Les Misérables movie thought they needed to add more than just the stage dimension, and it didn’t work.  Don’t get me wrong here - I think that Les Misérables would make an excellent movie!  An incredible movie, even!  But it has to be a movie - no singing!  Of course, the famous music could still be incorporated into a movie, and key parts could have a song incorporated into it.  But keep it a movie. And get Quentin Tarantino to direct it ;-)


One of the biggest strengths of the Les Misérables musical is that it is perfectly fitted to the musical medium, and this allows it to stir emotion throughout.  Movies can do that as well, but the dialog must be changed to fit the medium - in other words, Les Misérables would need to be adapted to a movie format to be an effective and stirring movie.


Unfortunately, the producers of the 2012 movie pumped out a movie-like musical film that was horrific at best.  I wouldn’t recommend it to anyone, regardless of whether they have seen the Les Misérables musical before. Instead, just go out and see the musical and enjoy the story in a way that it was meant to be portrayed!
