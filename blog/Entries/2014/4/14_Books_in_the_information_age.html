Books in the information age

Monday, April 14, 2014

 

How many of you still read physical books exclusively?

How many of you only read digital books (eBooks)?


Me?  I have a healthy mix of the two, and this blog post will tell you why.


Up until this past decade, and for the past several hundred years, we transferred knowledge primarily via printed books - from fun stories and serious literature, to educational textbooks and scientific research.  For those of us who were raised in an era without eBooks, printed books are considered a symbol of knowledge - a respected, and romantic icon that is good.  Very good.  We were raised looking at picture books with our parents.  We learned to read from printed books in our elementary school.  And we watched television shows and movies where books were depicted in various ways, sometimes mystical!


Of course, computers and the Information Age have radically changed how we can get the same information previously available within printed books.  I can buy an eBook version of nearly any new book (and many classics) for cheaper than the printed version, and have it within seconds across the Internet (versus driving to a bookstore or waiting for shipping from Amazon).   It makes sense to ditch printed books, right?  Well.....in my opinion, not exactly.....


New generations today are still exposed to printed books (we’re not a total digital society yet) - even my daughter reads printed books exclusively and refuses to read eBooks.  And for me, a mix of the two makes perfect, logical sense.


Firstly, lets look at the pros and cons of eBooks.

Pros:

  1. •Cheaper

  2. •Easier to obtain

  3. •Searchable by keyword

  4. •Don’t use up any bookshelf space at all!

Cons:

  1. •Not great for books that contain photos (unless it is a PDF, and even then a printed version is still a better form factor)

  2. •Not something that can be easily “leafed” through (for example, to show someone something)

  3. •Have no collectable value, and cannot be physically signed

  4. •Not available for all titles, or for many older titles published before 2000

For example, if you are an avid novel reader (i.e. you burn through 3-4 books per month or more), it makes very good sense to purchase eBooks exclusively rather than printed books.  Novels often don’t have any pictures, so they are well suited to eBook format (and portable eBook readers like the Amazon Kindle).  You won’t fill up bookshelves full of books, and you’ll save lots of money.  If you are the type of person who sells their book to a used book store after reading it for a small amount, you won’t be able to do that with an eBook, but then again, the $$ you get from the used book store will be less than the discount you got for buying an eBook (and you still get to keep the eBook).


Another good choice for eBooks are textbooks.  Since textbooks are used only for the course you are taking, you probably don’t want a physical book hanging around.  Plus, they are searchable!


I’m a big fan of Sci-Fi books, and most new Sci-Fi books I buy as eBooks.  There are some books, however, that are so incredibly good, that I want a physical copy of to put on my bookshelf or get signed (I only have one bookshelf). 


I’m also a big fan of technology, vintage computing and game books.  For these books, I either buy a physical book or read the eBook depending on the features - if the book is available in digital format (even a scan, if it is a vintage book), I’ll often get the digital copy, and if it is an amazing or collectible book, I’ll find a used copy online for my bookshelf.  Also, if the book is chalked full of pictures, I’ll order a physical copy for my bookshelf. 


BUT.....I have to be careful since I don’t want to have more than one bookshelf!  As a result, before I buy any physical book, I make sure I’ve first read a copy from a library or friend.  “Try before you buy” is more important when it comes to physical books! 


The same can apply to eBooks too.  For example, I have two Amazon Kindles - one I loan out to a friend if they want to read a particular book but aren’t sure if they want to buy it. 


Other friends of mine download the book from some torrent site on the net and read it - if they like it, they pay for a digital copy, and if they think it was a waste of time, they don’t (this is sort of like natural selection for books - it contributes to weeding out crap on the book market).


One important tip I have for eBook owners is:  Ensure that you have a backup copy of your eBook without DRM!   DRM (Digital Rights Management) puts restrictions on the book that you buy because publishers think that this curbs piracy, which is total bullshit.  What DRM means to an eBook owner is that you could be prevented from reading your eBook in the future if the DRM stuff goes wonky (e.g. your Kindle dies).  As a result, make sure you use a DRM-removal tool on all eBooks you buy that come with DRM (there is a plug-in for the free Calibre eBook Management Software that does this beautifully).  Next, back up your non-DRM’ed eBooks to at least a few places (external hard disk, DVD, Dropbox) so that you ensure that you don’t lose your eBook if your computer dies.


So what if you have a ton of books filling up bookshelves already? 

What I’d suggest doing is:

  1. 1.Pick the ones you really want to keep - they should easily fit on a single bookshelf with room for more.

  2. 2.For any books that you want to keep (perhaps because you’ll want to read them again), but that you don’t want to put on a bookshelf, buy an eBook of it if it is available.

  3. 3.Sell all of the books you don’t want on your bookshelf to a used bookstore (which will likely pay for the previous step plus more).
