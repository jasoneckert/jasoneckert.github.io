The age of technical stupidity

Saturday, June 18, 2011

 

We are in “A Gadget Age” - well, at least that is what Leo Laporte has called it.  I have a different name for it that you saw in this post’s title that I’ll explain now....


When you walk around today, people are carrying gadgets of all types: smartphones, tablets, iPods, GPSes, etc.   Moreover, new gadgets are all the rave - people eagerly await the next Apple product or cool gadget that will “allow you to do everything” (they even talk about it in their own social circles).


My step-brother Andrew is currently in medical school, and uses a MacBook to type notes from class.  It is much quicker than shorthand, which allows you to get everything the prof says, as well as easy to edit/revise afterwards.   He told me that many of his classmates are scribbling poor-looking notes on their iPads (because everyone MUST have an iPad) and then collating them at home.  This is what drawn notes look like on an iPad:



In short, the general public is well-gadgeted......but I wonder how well those gadgets are being used....... 


I’m a technologist, but I’m not a gadgeteer.  What is the difference?  Simple.  Technologists are always aware of the fact that a piece of technology is not important within itself - it is the expression of that technology that is important.  For example, I’m sure that the microbiologists who discovered Telomerase and its link to cancer were not thinking about the cool specs on the computers they used when tackling the problem.  Instead, for them, technology was a tool to achieve something important, and they simply found and used whatever was available at the time to solve a puzzle that consumed their thoughts.


Gadgeteers, on the other hand, believe that technology will allow them to do great things somehow.......sometime.......magically.   That is why they keep getting the latest and greatest gadget that does something previous gadgets don’t do.   Never mind the creativity.....


Unfortunately, gadgeteerism is so rampant in society today that it is considered a social norm.  And what do we have to show for it - a ton of people who express themselves based on the technology available at the time.  This is why you saw teenagers with personal cell phones a decade ago talking everywhere, and why you see teenagers today with smartphones texting each other everywhere.  They aren’t thinking about what they are doing - they are just doing it because they have the gadget.  View this sample “typical” dialog using smartphone texting and ask yourself whether you see these people doing anything important in the future such as discovering a cure for cancer:

It’s almost as if they are de-evolving (the English language is for sure ;-).

The real problem is that gadgeteerism promotes laziness and the “I want it now” mentality - both of which have also led to the general problem plaguing education today as I discussed in my “Math education reform” post last week.  Sites like Facebook and Twitter have arisen because of this social gadgeteerism and not because they allow us to solve particular problems.


In short, gadgeteerism is making society dumber.  So a better term for today’s age is “The Age of Technical Stupidity.”  Jaron Lanier (who coined “Virtual Reality”) makes similar points in his book “You Are Not A Gadget” (a good read if you want to explore this topic in more depth).
