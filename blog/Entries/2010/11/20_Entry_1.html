Trying to crack KRYPTOS

Saturday, November 20, 2010

 

For those who don’t know what KRYPTOS is (likely because you haven’t read Dan Brown ;-), it is a sculpture at the CIA that contains 4 encrypted messages.  The first three have been cracked back in the 1990s, but the last one is a bitch because it depends on clues left in the first three.  I bought the mini KRYPTOS replica from the Spy Museum website a while ago and sometimes like to take a stab at it when I need stress relief. 


Cracking any cryptography is a combination of several things: 

  1. Analysis - Looking at the problem in different ways to see if there is an interesting relationship or pattern.

  2. Research - Looking for relationships between the meaning of the deciphered data as well as the possible methods used to encrypt the data (in this case, a 16th-century method developed by a Frenchman).

  3. Modeling - Taking stabs at calculating data from what you have researched and analyzed earlier - for me, this usually involves mapping key things out on a spreadsheet and then writing a program to remap the data to see if it has any kind of meaning (or interesting relationships that hint that I am on the right track).  After modeling hundreds of times, you might get lucky and eventually crack the code - so you must be quite tenacious to like this kind of stuff.  And I am uber-tenacious (a fancy way of saying anti-social).


Anywho, the artist who designed the sculpture has recently been disappointed that no one has cracked KRYPTOS yet, so he released a hint today in The New York Times (the 20th anniversary of the sculpture).  You can find the hint here: http://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2010/11/20/us/code.html?ref=us


Of course, what the hell have I been doing all day?  Working on this thing, because I have no life and can prove it mathematically.  The hint was a big one - letters 64-69 decode to “BERLIN” - when you compare this to the original text, you will notice two original identical letters (T) that map to the last two letters in BERLIN.  This usually indicates a phase shift of the cipher.  After taking a look at possible phase shifts, I noticed that EVERY phase shift that is of interest is exactly 6 positions, including the purposeful typos in the previous 3 sections.  For example, iqlusion, which should be illusion - “q” is a 6 phase shift of the letter “l”.  So, I’ve been building some simulations today that will hopefully tell me more about this “6” relationship in the data.  I just started compiling the simulation and will run it soon and dump the results into SPSS tomorrow morning and analyze the results all day tomorrow. 


Yes, I have no life, but I like this kind of stuff - it’s food for the mind.
