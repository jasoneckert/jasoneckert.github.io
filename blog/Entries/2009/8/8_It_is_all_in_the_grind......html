It is all in the grind.....

Saturday, August 8, 2009

 

I am a coffee addict - this compliments my purpose in life, which is to transport large quantities of caffeine from the coffee maker to the urinal.


In addition, I am extremely keen on getting the “best” cup of coffee.  If I need to get coffee on the road, I’ll choose a Bold roast from Starbucks such as Gold Coast, Gazebo, or Sumatra, or a Cafe Americano (expresso).  Tim Hortons and McDonalds are too mellow for me, but I’ll drink it when I’m not in Starbucks country. 


At home, coffee making is a bit tougher.  For years, I thought the best coffee was made by the Starbucks Utopia vacuum coffee maker, which made excellent coffee (it didn’t burn off the oils in the coffee like regular coffee makers, thus preserving the entire flavor in the beans).   However, when my Starbucks Utopia broke, I learned that they didn’t make it anymore and a friend told me to try a steel Bodum, which French-presses coffee in much the same way that the Utopia did.  Low and behold, it was even better than the Utopia!


I would buy Starbucks coffee beans (Bold of course), grind them in my electric burr grinder (which gives a better grind than regular coffee grinders), dump the grinds into the steel Bodum, pour in water (just off boil), wait 4 minutes, press the steel filter down and pour the coffee.


However, this past week, I have learned how to make EVEN BETTER coffee! While I was in Algonquin Provincial Park (think middle of nature, no electricity, no humans, etc.) I had to grind my own beans without the electric burr grinder.  So, at the Lake Opeongo Outfitting store (where they sell gear for long and rugged canoe/camping trips), I bought a manual burr coffee grinder (see the picture above).  It is the Lexan JavaGrind, which is made of nearly indestructible resin, detaches and folds so it uses far less space in your backpack, and most importantly, has a beautiful ceramic burr grinding base (the brown part in the picture).


What I learned while getting in touch with nature is that the grinder makes a huge difference in the cup of coffee - it took me several tries to get it right, but I found that with a manual burr grinder, you can slow the grinding process down and speed it up in waves to get a more or less asymmetrical or “jagged” cut - this results in a mixture of rough, fine and very fine coffee grinds in the same batch of coffee grinds.  By using this method to grind 15-20% more Starbucks Bold roast coffee beans than you would normally use to make coffee, and then brewing them using a steel Bodum, you will get a most amazing flavorful cup of coffee!


You must try it.


Step 1: Buy the following:

•Lexan JavaGrind Portable Coffee Grinder with ceramic burr by GSI (about $30)
•Columbia Thermal Coffee Press By Bodum/Starbucks (about $60)
•Bold Starbucks coffee (Sumatra, Gold Coast, Sidamo, Gazebo, etc. - about $14/pound)

Step 2: Measure the amount of coffee beans recommended for the number of cups you are going to brew, and then add 15-20% more beans.


Step 3: Place the beans in the Lexan JavaGrind and close the slider.  Take the cover off the ceramic burr, and place the Lexan JavaGrind over your Columbia Thermal Coffee Press (or similar steel Bodum).  


Step 4:  Start very slowly grinding the beans (you will hear them crack apart).  After a few seconds, speed up the grind until you can’t hear the cracking (because it is too fast) and then slow back down again.  Repeat this process until the coffee is completely ground.  You can even do it to music, while chanting, or while performing a rain dance around a camp fire (kind of like summoning the coffee gods with an ancient indian ritual).


Step 5:  Remove the Lexan JavaGrind and look at the beans - they should look rough and not have a definite consistency.  Boil enough water for the number of cups of coffee you with to make.  Just before you get a rolling boil, pour the water into the Columbia Thermal Coffee Press and put the top on (do NOT press the plunger/filter yet!).


Step 6:  After waiting approximately 4 minutes, press the plunger/filter on the Columbia Thermal Coffee Press and pour the coffee.  Enjoy!


NOTE: I drink coffee black without milk/cream/sweetener.  If you are the type of person who likes milk/cream/sweetener in their coffee, try it first without and you will probably be hooked.  A good coffee is best drunk black.
