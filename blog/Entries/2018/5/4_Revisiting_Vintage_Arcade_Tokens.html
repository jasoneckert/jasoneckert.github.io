Revisiting Vintage Arcade Tokens

Friday, May 4, 2018

 

A while ago I managed to buy a bag of arcade tokens from someone at a collectibles show for $10.  After cleaning them up as much as I could, I added them to plastic coin sleeves and spent the next several weeks doing some detective work to find out where most of them came from!  It was a lot of fun, and gave me a great appreciation for some of the video arcades and other locations that hosted video arcade machines across North America.


When arcade machines became popular in the late 1970s, tokens followed suit.  You would buy tokens from an arcade operator or token-dispensing machine, and then use the tokens to insert credits into the game of your choice.  They were better than using quarters for both the arcade owner and the player.  Tokens cut down on theft (people would often break into unwatched arcade machines to steal quarters), and most places gave you 5 tokens for $1, which meant that you had an extra game for free compared to using quarters!


The coolest thing about tokens is that there were hundreds, if not thousands, of different kinds that identified the arcade venue you went to.  Moreover, arcade tokens had different characteristics for different types of arcade venues.  In this blog post, I’ll talk about the different features that arcade tokens had for dedicated video arcades, family fun centers, fun parks, and so on.

Family fun centers and fun parks were just as popular back in the 1980s when dedicated video arcades were everywhere - however, they didn’t rely on making their money from just the games - they often made most of their money from food and other events.  As a result, many of these locations are still operating today, including the well-known Chuck E. Cheese.  However, the majority of games you’ll find in these locations are redemption (ticket) games. Nearly all of these locations had a mascot (for brand recognition), and their arcade tokens often had their mascot on the token itself - for example, the Chuck E. In the 1980s, arcade machines could be found outside dedicated arcades, family fun centers and fun parks - you could find an arcade or two at your local convenience store, pizza parlour, movie theatre, grocery store or bar.  These were called “street locations” by arcade operators back in the 1980s, who often leased the machines to each location and/or split the revenue from them.  Street locations are still common today! Anywhere that provides entertainment often has an arcade machine or two, sometimes more - just check out your local movie theatre, like Cineplex, and you’ll find arcades and a token machine to buy tokens for them!  If the street location is big enough (e.g. a movie theatre chain like Cineplex), then you’ll find custom tokens with branding.  However, for smaller locations, they’ll often use tokens with a generic picture on them, such as the Statue of Liberty, a clown, or simply the word “Token”, as shown below:
