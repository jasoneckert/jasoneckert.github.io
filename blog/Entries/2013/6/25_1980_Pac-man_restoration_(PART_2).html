1980 Pac-man restoration (PART 2)

Tuesday, June 25, 2013

 

If you’ve read my previous post, you’ll know that I scored a working 1980 Pac-man cocktail arcade table in amazing condition, and that I’m working on restoring every little part of it so that it will easily last another 33 years.


Here is a quick recap of what I did in my last post: I took everything out of the arcade, redid the inside in yellow enamel, polished up all of the latches/braces, restored the metal grilles, as well as re-mounted the PCB, grilles and latches/braces again.


Since the last post, I’ve completely disassembled and cleaned the entire coin door assembly.  I then re-coated it, polished up all silver metal components, re-assembled it and bolted it back into place.  I also scrubbed the 25 cent plexiglass insert, and cleaned the plastic coin mechanism. 



Following this, I disassembled the metal control panels joysticks - there were a few rust spots near the rubber diaphragm and control panel lip, so I sanded those down and re-coated all of the metal parts, cleaned the plastic parts and reassembled them again.  The leaf switches and vinyl contact ball were in excellent shape, so I didn’t need to replace anything there.  And I also polished up the joystick shafts (which are made of copper as you can see below).  The only thing that you’ll notice in the finished picture (bottom right below) is that I have to replace the black t-molding.  T-molding is really cheap (39 cents per foot), but I just have to find the time to get up to the Playdium Store in Toronto to get some ;-)



The next thing I did was replace the 115VAC fan at the bottom.  The old fan was working fine, but was pretty gunky when I took it apart - so I ended up buying a brand new one, and I’m glad I did because the new one has better air flow and looks great as shown to the right. 


Since these fans just connect directly to the  A/C power, I thought that it would be good to mount a power drop (with breaker and surge protection) in the unused part of the case near the speaker and plug in the fan to it.  I can then plug in the CRT isolation transformer and PCB isolation transformer to it as well (which is very clean and “just makes sense”).   This is what the inside looks at this stage:



The only thing remaining to do now is re-add the wiring (after cleaning it), transformers (which I already cleaned up - see my isolation transformer in the picture to the right), fuses, and CRT monitor.  It may seem like a lot of work, but it will actually be quite easy - I’m going to use plastic spiral wrap tubing on the wiring bundles so it looks nice and clean and make sure they are secured to the case using new cable fasteners.


Then, I’ll turn it on and play some Pac-man for several hours ;-)


Stay tuned for Part 3, where I showcase the finished arcade!
