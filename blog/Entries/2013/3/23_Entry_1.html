The Intel Pentium turns 20 years old

Saturday, March 23, 2013

 
The world’s most famous microprocessor brand, the Intel Pentium, turned 20 years old yesterday!  It was the famous successor to the Intel 486, and so people called the Pentium the “586” for many years afterwards as a result.  The first Pentium ran at 60MHz and was incredibly expensive - the right picture is my 60MHz Pentium, which I kept for purely nostalgic reasons.


The Intel Pentium became a household term overnight partially because of Intel’s aggressive marketing, but also because of timing.


Intel plastered every PC that had a Pentium processor with a sticker that said something along the lines of Intel Inside - Pentium, and this practice continued with the word Pentium until only about 5 years ago, when the majority of Intel PC stickers switched to something like “Core 2 Duo inside” or  “Core i7 inside.”  And Intel made sure that there were plenty of magazine adverts and TV commercials to follow suit.  But for about 15 years straight, the word “Pentium” was synonymous with “PC” for most people.  And even today, people know what Pentium means.


This past year, I ended up going to McDonalds to have a little fun:

Cashier:  I’m sorry?

Me:  A McBistro Chicken BLT please.

Cashier: The meal?

Me:  No, just the sandwich please.

Cashier: Anything else?

Me: No thanks.

Cashier: That will be $5.86

Me: See?

Cashier: Oh my god.....


Timing was perhaps the biggest reason why the Pentium was successful - after all, it came out at the time when the PC business exploded and the Internet came to dominance.  Although it was introduced March 22, 1993, it really never made its way into homes and businesses until about 1995, when Windows 95 was introduced.  Windows 95 was a revolutionary new thing back then, and was many people’s first computer.  It also led to businesses moving towards computing like never before.  Windows 95 was the future, and you ran it on the Intel Pentium.


Of course, introducing the Pentium two years earlier in 1993 was a good thing, because there are always a few early adopters - usually businesses or academic institutions that have the need for a powerful workstation to serve a specific purpose and who are willing to pay a premium price for it.   These early adopters managed to find the FDIV bug that was fixed by the time Windows 95 came out - you can read about the FDIV bug here: <click to read my blog post about the FDIV bug>


I’ll always consider the Pentium a pivotal point in computing history - it was the processor that powered the Internet revolution and became a household term.  For us older geeks, we’ll also remember it as the first processor that was important enough to have a name, and something that everyone with a 386 or 486 at home in the early 1990s secretly (or not so secretly) wanted to have ;-)
