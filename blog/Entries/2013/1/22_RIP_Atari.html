RIP Atari

Tuesday, January 22, 2013

 
The Internet is full of news today about how Atari and their French parent company have declared bankruptcy (actually, they are seeking bankruptcy protection, which is fundamentally the same thing - few companies every recover from it).


But why is everyone all upset about it?  

They are upset because Atari won our hearts and minds in the 1970s and 1980s with their amazing arcade games and home consoles. 


Atari made the first popular arcade game (Pong), and followed with some of the most memorable arcade games of all time: Breakout, Asteroids, Centipede, Tempest, Gauntlet, Missile Command, Tetris, Star Wars, and many more.

 

And lest we forget the amazing Atari 2600 home console that  had a bazillion games available for it.  Everyone had one prior to the Nintendo.  Today, pictures of the Atari 2600 controller are the standard icon of home video gaming from that era.




In other  words, Atari is really the grandfather of video games - Atari made the arcade scene happen, and they were the ones that popularized home video game consoles.  


That is why people are upset at the recent news.  Nobody likes to hear a great inventor of a lifestyle-changing technology die, whether it be a person like Steve Jobs, or an inanimate company like Atari.  A little piece of us dies inside.


Many people from my generation have fond memories of video game arcades, and playing games on our Atari 2600 consoles growing up.  Those of younger generations have a respect for the company that put video games on the map and inevitably led to the enjoyment we get from them today.  Regardless, every gamer knows what Atari is - Atari is where it all began.


But let’s distance ourselves from those notions for now.  What is Atari, really?  It’s just a company started by Nolan Bushnell in 1972 (Nolan is an awesome person, who is still alive and someone you want to hear speak on Youtube).  He sold it to Warner in 1976, who took the Atari 2600 and the first few Atari PCs (the 400 and 800) to massive popularity.  Warner sold it to the original founder of Commodore, Jack Tramiel, after Jack left Commodore.  Jack thought Atari should be a PC company, and he ran it into the ground (Atari PCs in the late 1980s and 1990s?  Give me a break.).  Tramiel sold it to Hasbro, who kept it for a few years for the name and then sold it to Infogrames in France, who really didn’t do much with it other than re-release a few of their old games for the iPad.  Few people after the mid 1990s thought that Atari was still around (see the timeline at the top of this post).


Atari has essentially been out of the gaming business for the past 20 years - what they’ve produced on the gaming side has been very weak - a handful of largely unknown arcade games in the 1990s (mostly subcontracted to Midway), and no real hits for modern platforms after that.   I’m surprised they lasted this long.


But Atari is a cultural icon that will last forever - in people’s minds, they will always be the imaginary cosmic force that gave birth to the video game industry.  Because of this, Atari is a very powerful brand.  I think that some company may just snatch up the rights to the brand and carry the torch.  And if they actually make some good games under that brand, people will flock to them like a bunch of scruffy nerfherders to a Star Wars flick.
