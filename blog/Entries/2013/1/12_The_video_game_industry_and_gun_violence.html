The video game industry and gun violence

Saturday, January 12, 2013

 

Joe Biden has announced that he wants to meet with video game developers to discuss how to deal with gun violence in the United States.  This is essentially the same as wanting to meet with Mattel, the makers of Hot Wheels toys, to discuss car safety for the automotive industry.


Essentially, it’s a retarded mix of:

  1. a) People not understanding what causes gun violence in the US, and
    b) Wanting to ride a historically popular scapegoat that makes billions of dollars

Now THAT sounds like something appealing for a politician ;-)  But realistically, it’s more like this comic:



If the US wants to understand why they have a gun problem, they just need to look at any other country in the world....say, for example, us hosers in Canada.  We are right next to the US, and have an identical culture and similar economy.   We grew up watching the same shows, listening to the same music, going to McDonalds, and playing the same video games.  The only difference between us hosers and Americans, is that we weren’t brought up generation-over-generation thinking that we had the right to have a gun. 


So why pin gun violence on video games?


Well, since the 1970s, the luddites have heavily criticized video games because new things are scary.  One town in Texas even tried to get the game Space Invaders made illegal because so many kids in the town were playing the game (luckily, they didn’t succeed).  But not everyone was completely retarded back then either - check out the old news clip at the bottom of this post (watch it until the end).  The news team does a good job of showing both sides in the correct light.  While this news clip is 30 years old, everything in it still applies to video games today. 


At their core, video games are simply games.  Games that are played on some kind of computer that has controls and a video display, whether it be an old arcade machine, or a game console, home computer, mobile tablet or smartphone.  And we’ve been playing games (violent and non-violent) for thousands of years.  In this particular situation, “the medium is not the message.” (my apologies, Marshall McLuhan ;-)
