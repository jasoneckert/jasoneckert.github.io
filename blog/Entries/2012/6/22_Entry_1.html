Pac-man is no longer 8-bit....it is 64-bit

Friday, June 22, 2012

 

Yup - that is right - but bear with me, because it will take me some time to explain....


Firstly, I’m a big fan of video arcade games - I grew up in the 8-bit era of video games in the 1980s.  They call it 8-bit because the printed circuit boards (PCBs) that contained the actual arcade game used an 8-bit CPU such as a Zilog Z80, Motorola 6809, or MOS 6502.  These primitive CPUs are what generated the equally primitive graphics and gameplay that rocked 1980s arcades.


Pac-man is no exception - it is defined by its “cute” simplicity and “wocka wocka” sound effects.  Similarly, Galaga is defined by its straightforward and fun gameplay.


In the 1980s, both Pac-man and Galaga were comprised of an arcade cabinet that held a monitor and controls that were connected to a PCB that had a Zilog Z80 CPU (Galaga actually had 3 of these CPUs). 


Over the 1980s, there were several 8-bit spin-offs of both Pac-man and Galaga (e.g. Ms Pac-man, Super Pac-man, Baby Pac-man, Pac-man Plus, Galaga 3, Galaga ’88, etc.).  But by 1990, the fad had worn off, and people were interested in games that had more impressive 16-bit and 32-bit graphics.


The funny thing is that both Pac-man and Galaga were still hot sellers during the 1990s in arcades because they were such great games - people who didn’t like fancy video games still enjoyed them.  And Namco (the company that made both games) sold more units. 


Because of this, Namco released the 20th Anniversary Edition Ms Pac-man/Galaga arcade in 2001 (see picture to the right).   It was very popular, and more importantly, it was true to its 8-bit roots since the PCB (shown below) ran a Zilog Z180 CPU. 




The Zilog Z180 was simply a speed-enhanced Z80 - as a result, it could run the original 8-bit game code.  All Namco had to do was write a bit of code to fill in the other functions, such as choosing whether to play Pac-man or Galaga.  It made perfect sense from a development perspective, and it was still 8-bits in every way.


Needless to say that the Ms Pac-man/Galaga arcade was wildly popular, and Namco followed up with a 25th Anniversary Edition 5 year later using almost the exact same PCB and Z180 CPU.


However, that changed with the 30th Anniversary Editions this year.  Namco released two arcades to celebrate their 30th Anniversary:

1) Pac-man Battle Royale - A four-player sit-down version of Pac-man (very cool).
2) Pac-man Arcade Party (30th Anniversary Edition) - An arcade that had 12 Namco classics: Pac-man, Galaga, Pac-mania, Galaxian, Rally X, Mappy, Dig Dug, Xevious, Bosconian, Galaga ’88, Rolling Thunder, and Dragon Spirit

Naturally, chip nerds like me love to see what kind of guts are in the thing, but there was very little information available about either system.  So, I resorted to the Internet.  After long searching, I found an unboxing of Pac-man Battle Royale that showed the PCBs in the thing - they were definitely more complex than the single small Z180 PCB used previously, and there was a fan over the CPU!




The problem is that the picture wasn’t high enough resolution to read some of the chips.....dead end......until yesterday when I found someone on eBay selling a Pac-man Arcade Party PCB set pulled from a new arcade for $900 - and yes, there were some nice, hi-res photos in there that showed me enough of the chips that I could make out that two of them were Sony-branded CPUs and that the PCB was labelled Namco System147.  So I searched for System147, and ended up on a Japanese web site.  Although Google’s translate wasn’t perfect, it clearly said that these systems used a smaller slimmed-down version of Namco’s System246.  Namco System246 is essentially the exact same hardware in a Sony Playstation 2, and was popular for some arcades in the early 2000s such as Tekken 4.  Moreover, it said that System147 was used for both Pac-man Battle Royale AND Pac-man Arcade Party arcades.


Now, Namco System147 is much newer than 2000, so I imagined the hardware to be very similar to a Playstation 2 Slim (a later version released around 2005).  I Googled a picture of the Playstation 2 Slim PCB and BAM!  The chips were identical (by label and by size), and were in almost the same layout.  The top two pics below are from the eBay listing for the Pac-man Arcade Party PCB, and the bottom one is from a modern Playstation 2:




What that means is that under that cooling fan and blue silicon pad on the Pac-man Arcade Party PCB is a flat silver chip that is essentially the Sony Playstation 2 Emotion Engine CPU+GPU (on a single chip).  And this chip is a 64-bit MIPS R5900 CPU.......a far cry from the old 8-bit CPU of the Pac-man games up until now. 


However, it makes perfect sense for Namco to do this - the hardware is easy to produce, and the Playstation 2 development tools are easy to use to adapt the games to it.


So the next time you are in an arcade and you see someone playing the latest Pac-man Arcade Party 30th Anniversary Edition game, raving about how they love 8-bit games, you can correct them and let them know that they are actually playing a 64-bit game ;-)
