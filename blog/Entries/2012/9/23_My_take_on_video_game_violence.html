My take on video game violence

Sunday, September 23, 2012

 

I’m a dean for a faculty that teaches video game development.  What that means is that I’m frequently talking about the video game industry at various events.  I’ve attended an unusually large number of events these past few months, and found myself addressing the subject of video game violence several times because someone brought it up.  Until this past month, I thought that very few people still blamed video games for violent behavior in society.  And if that is the case, I managed to meet those few who still think video games are evil.


This post summarizes my viewpoints on video game violence and society, and is roughly what I say when someone brings up the subject.   That being said, those people I talked to these past few months who thought that video games were evil didn’t seem to acknowledge what I was saying, for whatever reason.  Their mind was already made up, and nothing could change it.


It isn’t hard to point a finger at video games when it comes to violence - many video games that people play today (and historically) have demonstrated some sort of violence, whether it be blowing up enemy ships, killing zombies, or enemy soldiers.


However, we must remind ourselves that we live in a violent society.  It has been like that for all of human history, and isn’t likely going to change anytime soon.   If all video games were non-violent, then the video game industry probably wouldn’t exist - people would search out other venues for entertainment that are more realistic within today’s societal context.  Remember that people play games for a variety of different reasons, and not simply because they are a novelty.


I’m a video game player myself, and have been for as long as I can remember - from video arcades, to home video game consoles, to PC and online games.  Nearly everyone I grew up with plays video games, and I’ve played video games with hundreds, perhaps even thousands of different people over the years.  We are now affectionately called gamers.


For gamers, the violence in video games is pure fantasy - it’s not real, and doesn’t get translated into violence in the real world.  There are some exceptions to this of course, but those crackpots could just as easily translate the violence from movies, television, or literature into the real world.  Making video games non-violent won’t prevent some crackpot from gunning down a theatre full of people - some people just snap.  Sometimes, reality can suck.


Many gamers I know primarily play games as a means to compete with other gamers.  Others primarily play it for mental stimulation or to pass time in a mentally-stimulating way.  Most people I know primarily play video games to blow off steam and relax.  in a way, violent video games give people a way to vent frustration and anger in a non-harmful way.   This was one of the key reasons why youth of my generation spent time in video arcades, and why many people play violent video games today.  Society can be stressful.


Today, people of all ages play video games, and they have a smorgasbord of game genres to choose from (e.g. puzzle, adventure, social, educational, role-play, fantasy, sports, campaign, and shooter games).  Plus, many games don’t have violence at all.  Thus, people can choose to play video games to fill whatever need they need to fill.  If they need to blow off steam, they’ll likely choose a violent game.  Alternatively, if they are looking for a mental challenge, they’ll probably choose a game that doesn’t have violence at all!


It is also important to note that video games don’t teach us how to be violent - society and movies do an excellent job of that.  Moreover, there is no long-term impact when blowing off steam by playing a violent video game - when the steam is gone, the player stops playing the game and moves on to something else.  


So, in short, my take on video game violence is actually quite simple:  Bring it, bitches!
